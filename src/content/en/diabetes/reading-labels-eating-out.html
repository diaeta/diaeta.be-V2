Reading Labels, Shopping, and Eating Out

Updated: 2025-09-13

On this page
  • Decoding the Nutrition Label
  • Strategies for Eating Out

Key Takeaways

  • Reading nutrition labels is a skill that empowers you to understand how a food will impact your blood sugar before you eat it.
  • Focus on the "Total Carbohydrate" line, and also note the "Dietary Fiber" and "Sugars" to assess the quality of the carbs.
  • When eating out, you can still make balanced choices by applying the principles of the balanced plate: look for lean proteins, healthy fats, and lots of non-starchy vegetables.
  • Don't be afraid to ask for modifications, such as sauces on the side or swapping fries for a salad, to better suit your health needs.

Decoding the Nutrition Label

The nutrition facts label is one of the most powerful tools you have for managing diabetes. While it contains a lot of information, you can quickly get the hang of it by focusing on a few key areas. This skill helps you move from guessing to making informed choices at the grocery store.

What to Look For:

  • Serving Size: All the numbers on the label apply to one serving. Be sure to check how many servings are in the package, as it's often more than one.
  • Total Carbohydrate: This is the most important number for diabetes management, as it represents the total amount of starches, sugars, and fiber that will affect your blood sugar.
  • Dietary Fiber: Found under Total Carbohydrate, fiber is your friend. It slows down sugar absorption. A higher fiber content is generally a good sign.
  • Total Sugars: This includes both natural and added sugars. While it's part of the Total Carbohydrate, it's wise to choose products with lower amounts of "Added Sugars" whenever possible.
  • Protein and Fat: Checking these can help you plan for a balanced meal. As you've learned, pairing carbs with protein and fat helps stabilize blood sugar.

Strategies for Eating Out

Eating out is a part of life and should be enjoyable, not stressful. By applying the same principles of balanced eating, you can navigate almost any menu with confidence.

Tips for Success:

  • Plan Ahead: If possible, look at the menu online before you go. This gives you time to think about your options without feeling rushed.
  • Think "Balanced Plate": Look for meals that include a lean protein (grilled fish, chicken), plenty of non-starchy vegetables (salads, steamed veggies), and a source of healthy fat (avocado, olive oil dressing).
  • Portion Control: Restaurant portions are often very large. Don't feel obligated to finish everything. Consider asking for a half-portion, sharing a main course, or packing up half to take home before you even start eating.
  • Be Smart About Carbs: Be mindful of large portions of high-carb sides like bread baskets, fries, or white rice. You can often swap these for extra vegetables or a side salad.
  • Ask for Modifications: Don't hesitate to ask for what you need. Most restaurants are happy to accommodate requests like putting sauces or dressings on the side, grilling or baking instead of frying, or swapping a side dish.
